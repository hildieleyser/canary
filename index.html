Canary Labs

Olfaction as a hidden-state interface between body, brain, environment, and behaviour.

Smell is how nervous systems read airborne chemistry and turn it into emotion, threat detection, memory, and action. Canary Labs studies that channel and builds the instrument to measure it. The same sensing logic points at three domains. Pick one to see the work.

Select a domain to see the work. The near-nose platform is the shared instrument across all three.

Domain one · the applied edge

Global health

Measure what a person actually breathes, closer to the body than any room sensor, and reduce it at the same time. This is where the platform starts, because the exposure is urgent, measurable, and testable today.

01

The first test case

Why dirty air first

Polluted air is a strong place to start because it is urgent, measurable, and biologically meaningful. The exposures are real and continuous, and sensors can read parts of them today without sampling any tissue.

8.1M

Deaths a year are attributed to air pollution worldwide. It is one of the largest environmental contributors to ill health, and most of it is invisible at the point where people breathe it.

State of Global Air 2024 · HEI / IHME / UNICEF

The chemical air people breathe is a mix of contributors, and a near-nose reading is meant to capture the part that actually reaches a person:

Particulate matterUltrafine particlesCombustion products VOCsAllergensSmokeDustMouldIndustrial fumes

These exposures may matter for respiratory disease, asthma, and other inflammatory conditions. They could also matter for child development, learning, and attention, and a growing body of work tests whether they contribute to long-term brain health. These are open questions the work might inform, not effects this device measures.

The measurement gap

Measured too far away

Air quality is usually reported at the room, street, or city level. That is useful for policy and for averages, but the biological question is what reaches the breathing zone. A child near traffic, a worker near fumes, or someone in a smoky room can have a very different exposure from the room average, and that difference is exactly the air that reaches the nose and lungs.

The neuroscience underneath

Olfaction as an interface

What makes smell unusual is its wiring. Most senses are relayed through the thalamus before they reach awareness. Olfaction has a faster, more direct route into limbic structures, which is part of why an odour can shift affect before it is consciously named. Reading the chemical air is a way to study that mapping, and it is the principle the other two domains build on.

On the research side, the longer aim is a model that takes the chemical reading together with simple context, such as scene and stated intent, and infers a person's likely state. That is a way to ask how much of affect and arousal is predictable from the air at the nose. It stays on the reading side of the problem. No odour is delivered, and no clinical state is diagnosed.

Experiment one

The first pilot

The first experiment places the three sensor positions side by side under safe indoor-air conditions and asks whether the near-nose reading differs from the room and purifier readings, and whether those differences track what people report.

Conditions compared

  • Room-level air sensor
  • Consumer air purifier or fan readout
  • Near-nose sensor reading
  • Filtered condition
  • Unfiltered condition

Basic readouts

  • Breathing comfort
  • Irritation
  • Headache
  • Fatigue
  • Smell test
  • Short attention task
  • Possibly simple non-invasive physiology

No deliberate exposure. The pilot does not expose people to harmful air on purpose. It tests the device under safe indoor-air conditions and compares readings across sensor positions. Any difference between filtered and unfiltered conditions comes from ordinary indoor air, not from introduced pollutants.

Domain two · sensing and minds

Animal welfare

Detect distress and illness earlier, without restraint or invasive monitoring, and read the pre-verbal state animals already broadcast in chemistry. Sensing and brain-computer interfaces sit side by side here as two pillars of the same question.

02

Why animals first show it in chemistry

The signal before the symptom

Animals often signal distress, illness, fear, pain, and social stress chemically and bodily long before any of it is visible in behaviour. Welfare is usually judged by what we can see, so suffering tends to be caught late. Reading the chemical layer could move that detection earlier, without restraint, blood tests, implants, or handling that is itself stressful.

The welfare opportunity

Earlier, and hands-off

A chemical reading does not need the animal to perform a behaviour, hold still, or be sampled. It can run in the background of an enclosure or near the muzzle. That makes it suited to settings where handling is hard, rare, or harmful, and where a daily visual check misses the early hours of a problem.

The strongest claim is plain: chemical sensing may let us detect suffering earlier than visual observation, before it becomes obvious.

The deeper question

Reading the wrong signals

Consciousness in animals is often judged through human-like behaviour. Many species may express meaningful internal states chemically, socially, and bodily rather than in anything that looks like language.

If that is true, we may be missing part of animal mind because we are reading the wrong channel. Pairing chemical sensing with brain-computer interfaces is a way to test how much internal state is legible once we stop relying on behaviour alone.

Pillar one

VOCs as welfare signals

Bodies emit volatile organic compounds, and the mix shifts with internal state. In animals that can mean pain, fear, distress, social tension, reproductive state, or early illness. Trained dogs already detect disease odours in humans, which is direct evidence that these signals carry usable information; the aim here is to read a useful part of them with sensors instead.

States that have been linked to changes in emitted volatile signals or body chemistry:

Acute stressFearPainInflammationInfection Metabolic changeReproductive stateSocial tensionEarly illness

What this is and is not. The aim is to read welfare-relevant change in the chemical signal, not to diagnose a named disease in an animal. The disease-odour literature is cited as evidence that volatile signals carry internal-state information, not as a claim that this device identifies specific conditions.

Pillar one, applied

Near-nose sensing for animals

The same sensing logic as the human platform, repositioned to an animal's breathing zone or to the air of its enclosure. No electronics go inside the animal, and no restraint is required. The reading is compared against the ambient air of the space, the same near versus room logic used on the human side.

enclosure air

Housing & labs

A sensing pod on a cage or pen tracks the chemical baseline of a group and flags drift, including social stress in group-housed animals.

transport & shelter

Movement & intake

Transport and shelter intake are high-stress, high-illness moments. Continuous chemical readings could catch trouble between the scheduled checks.

near the muzzle

Veterinary & sanctuary

A near-muzzle reading aims at one animal's breathing zone, for closer monitoring of pain or illness without sedation or handling.

Pillar two

BCIs and pre-verbal state

Brain-computer interfaces give a second window onto internal state. Read together with the chemical signal and with behaviour, and tracked over time, they let us ask what an animal is in rather than only what it is doing. The target is pre-verbal meaning: the units of state an animal acts on before anything that resembles language.

Read this way, interspecies communication becomes a tractable question. Not full translation, and not animal speech as human words, but detecting state, intent, stress, recognition, and social meaning from the signals animals already use. That could change how we study animals, how we care for them, and how we weigh their minds.

Domain three · a missing sense

AI safety

Give AI a sense of smell. Most systems sense vision, sound, text, and reward, and stay blind to the chemical world. A chemical state channel could let embodied AI read danger and internal state it currently cannot see.

03

The gap

The missing channel

Most AI learns from what is visible: images, text, audio, and reward signals. It infers internal and environmental state from surface behaviour, which can be shallow. Real animals and humans live in a chemical world. Smoke, infection, decay, fear, stress, food, and danger all have chemical signatures, and robots sense airborne chemistry far worse than they sense vision and sound.

What it adds

State a camera cannot see

A chemical channel reports on conditions that vision and audio miss entirely or catch late. For an embodied system, that is the difference between modelling context and guessing it from appearance.

Signals a chemical channel could surface:

SmokeGas leakDecayContaminationIllness Spoiled foodChemical threatAir qualityStress markers

Context, not appearance

Modelling the room

A system that smells smoke before it sees flame, or detects a gas leak with no visual cue, is modelling the environment rather than predicting it from surfaces. That is a more grounded basis for a safe action.

Robotics

Where bodies meet air

Robots already carry vision and sound. Olfactory sensors extend them to inspection, hazard detection, and search-and-rescue, where the decisive signal is often in the air rather than in the frame.

The model

Olfaction, vision, action

The research direction is a model that reads the chemical signal together with the scene and the task, and uses it to choose what to do. The near-nose platform is the sensor that produces the chemical half of that input, the same instrument used in the other two domains.

Scope, stated plainly. This does not claim to solve AI safety. It tests one thing: whether hidden biological and environmental state can be measured and used by AI systems. The strongest claim is that olfaction could become a missing state channel for safer embodied AI.
